## 2. Context

KMS Lighthouse is an enterprise knowledge management platform used by companies like DHL, GE Healthcare, AIG, and Allianz to power their call centers, self-service portals, and internal knowledge bases.

The Help Center is one of Lighthouse's external products. It lets businesses spin up a fully branded self-service site for their end users — pulling content directly from the Lighthouse platform their teams already use, so no separate site, no data migration, no parallel content workflow.

It's sold separately from the core platform, but built to extend it. For existing customers it's an upsell; for new ones it's a differentiator against competitors who can't offer the same end-to-end loop.

---

## 3. Role & Approach

I was the sole designer on this project, working alongside product managers and engineers across multiple delivery phases.

That meant owning every part of the design — research, IA, flows, wireframes, UI, design system contributions, and handoff. It also meant balancing two very different audiences inside one product: the business admin configuring the Help Center, and the end user trying to find an answer fast.

---

## 4. The Problem

Lighthouse had an existing Help Center product, but it wasn't selling.

The reasons were structural, not cosmetic:

**Complex setup.** Every change — from copy to layout to branding — had to be made in the backend by a specialized dev team. Admins couldn't touch their own site.

**Usability issues.** Most end users accessed help on mobile, but the existing design wasn't responsive. Articles were hard to find, support tickets went up instead of down, and the whole experience felt dated.

**Limited customization.** The product offered almost no flexibility. Businesses couldn't make the Help Center feel like their own brand, which made it a hard sell to enterprise clients who care about brand consistency.

The result: a product positioned as a competitive differentiator that wasn't actually differentiating anything. Existing clients passed on the upsell. New prospects went elsewhere.

---

## 5. Goals

Three goals shaped the redesign, each tied directly to one of the problems above:

**Put admins in control.**
Give business admins full ownership of their Help Center — setup, content, and maintenance — without needing dev or support involvement.

**Design mobile-first.**
Rebuild the end-user experience around how people actually use it: on their phone, looking for one answer, fast.

**Make it brandable.**
Give admins meaningful control over branding and layout so the Help Center could feel like a natural extension of their own site, not a third-party tool bolted on.

---

## 6. Research

To ground the redesign in real needs rather than assumptions, I ran four research tracks in parallel:

- **Stakeholder interviews** with business customers, focused on setup pain points, customization needs, and desired features.
- **User interviews** with both admins and end users, surfacing where the existing Help Center broke down on each side.
- **Competitive analysis** of other self-service help platforms, identifying both table-stakes patterns and opportunities to differentiate.
- **Surveys** with both audiences to validate qualitative findings at scale.

---

## 7. Key Insights

Three insights drove every design decision that followed:

**Brand customization isn't a nice-to-have.**
Enterprise clients won't ship a Help Center that doesn't feel like theirs. Layout, color, typography, content presentation — admins need real control, and they need it without writing code.

**Navigation is the product.**
For end users, the Help Center *is* its search and category structure. Everything else is decoration. Getting findability right was the single biggest unlock.

**The UI needed to earn its place.**
The old product looked like an admin tool. The new one needed to look like something a brand would proudly link from their main site.

## 10. The Admin Experience

The admin side is where this product had to earn its keep. If admins couldn't set up and customize the Help Center themselves, nothing else mattered.

I designed the admin panel as a series of focused settings: site configuration, localization, content management, and a dedicated customization panel for branding.

**Settings**
A dedicated section for managing the Help Center's website settings, language support, and integration with the Lighthouse platform — including real-time content sync.

*[Images 11, 12, 13, 14, 15]*

**Customization panel**
The customization panel gives admins direct control over their site's brand and structure: logo, favicon, header, footer, color system, card styles, and content layout. Non-technical users can change how the Help Center looks and behaves without touching a line of code.

*[Image 26 — the customization panel breakdown]*

**Homepage setup with guided onboarding**
First-time admins are walked through localization, language management, and translation workflows via inline guides — turning what was previously a developer task into a self-serve flow.

*[Images 17–22 — the onboarding/setup screens]*

## 11. The End-User Experience

For end users, success is one thing: finding the right answer fast.

The redesigned homepage leads with a hero search anchored in the brand's own visual identity, followed by category cards that map to the most common end-user tasks. Articles live in a clean reading layout with related links, helpful/not-helpful feedback, and step-by-step content where it applies.

# Notes for implementation

**On length.** The above is roughly 850 words of body copy. On a long-scroll web case study at typical reading pace, that's about a 4–5 minute read — appropriate for the depth without overstaying. If your skeleton has tighter sections, trim from Research and Wireframing first; lead with Hero, Problem, Goals, Admin, End-User, and Outcome.

**On images.** I've noted suggested images by their original deck number. Recommended priority if you need to cut:
- **Must keep:** 36 (final homepage), 33 (responsive), 26 (customization panel), 8 (user flows)
- **Strong supporting:** 11–15 (admin settings), 40 (article view), 9 (wireframes)
- **Cut first:** the bigger wireframe grid, the onboarding sequence (17–22) — these can be one composite image rather than five

**On the before/after.** If you can isolate the old Help Center screen (image 3) and pair it with the new one (image 36), that's the single most powerful recruiter moment in this case study. Strongly consider it.

**On client naming.** I've used "an airline customer" rather than naming Cebu Pacific. If the Help Center actually shipped with Cebu Pacific and you have approval to credit them, swap it back in — named enterprise clients are a major credibility signal.

**Two things still to circle back on:**
1. The Outcome section needs real content — even directional phrasing locked in.
2. The Reflection — one honest sentence on what you learned or would do differently.
